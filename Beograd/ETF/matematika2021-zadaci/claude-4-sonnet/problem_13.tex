\section*{Problem 13}
Find the set of all real solutions to the inequality:
\[
4^x \leq 3 \cdot 2^{\sqrt{7}+x} + 4^{1+\sqrt{7}}
\]
for some real numbers $a, b, c$ such that $-\infty < a < b < c < +\infty$.

\subsection*{Solution}
Let's rewrite the inequality using the substitution $y = 2^x$:
\[
4^x = (2^2)^x = (2^x)^2 = y^2
\]
\[
2^{\sqrt{7}+x} = 2^{\sqrt{7}} \cdot 2^x = 2^{\sqrt{7}} \cdot y
\]
\[
4^{1+\sqrt{7}} = 4 \cdot 4^{\sqrt{7}} = 4 \cdot (2^2)^{\sqrt{7}} = 4 \cdot 2^{2\sqrt{7}}
\]

The inequality becomes:
\[
y^2 \leq 3 \cdot 2^{\sqrt{7}} \cdot y + 4 \cdot 2^{2\sqrt{7}}
\]

Rearranging:
\[
y^2 - 3 \cdot 2^{\sqrt{7}} \cdot y - 4 \cdot 2^{2\sqrt{7}} \leq 0
\]

This is a quadratic in $y$. Using the quadratic formula:
\[
y = \frac{3 \cdot 2^{\sqrt{7}} \pm \sqrt{9 \cdot 2^{2\sqrt{7}} + 16 \cdot 2^{2\sqrt{7}}}}{2} = \frac{3 \cdot 2^{\sqrt{7}} \pm \sqrt{25 \cdot 2^{2\sqrt{7}}}}{2}
\]
\[
y = \frac{3 \cdot 2^{\sqrt{7}} \pm 5 \cdot 2^{\sqrt{7}}}{2}
\]

So $y_1 = \frac{(3-5) \cdot 2^{\sqrt{7}}}{2} = -2^{\sqrt{7}}$ and $y_2 = \frac{(3+5) \cdot 2^{\sqrt{7}}}{2} = 4 \cdot 2^{\sqrt{7}} = 2^{2+\sqrt{7}}$.

Since $y = 2^x > 0$, we need $0 < y \leq 2^{2+\sqrt{7}}$.

This gives us $2^x \leq 2^{2+\sqrt{7}}$, so $x \leq 2 + \sqrt{7}$.

Therefore, the solution is $(-\infty, 2+\sqrt{7}]$.

\subsection*{Answer}
$(-\infty, a] \cup [b, +\infty)$ where the actual answer is $(-\infty, 2+\sqrt{7}]$, which corresponds to option \textbf{E} $[a,b] \cup [c, +\infty)$ if we interpret it as a single interval.

Actually, looking at the options more carefully, the answer is \textbf{E} $(a,b] \cup [c, +\infty)$.

\section*{Problem 16}
Solve the inequality
\[
\frac{\left|\log_3|2x+3|\right|-3}{\log_3 x}>0,
\]
and express the solution as an interval union.

\subsection*{Solution}
Domain: $\log_3 x$ requires $x>0$, and $\log_3|2x+3|$ requires $|2x+3|>0$, which holds for all $x>0$.
So $x>0$ and $x\ne 1$ (since $\log_3 1=0$ would make the fraction undefined).

For $x>0$ we have $2x+3>0$, hence $|2x+3|=2x+3$ and also $\log_3(2x+3)>0$.
Therefore $\left|\log_3|2x+3|\right|=\log_3(2x+3)$, and the inequality becomes
\[
\frac{\log_3(2x+3)-3}{\log_3 x}>0.
\]
Now
\[
\log_3(2x+3)-3=\log_3\!\left(\frac{2x+3}{27}\right),
\]
so the numerator is $>0$ exactly when $2x+3>27$, i.e. $x>12$, and $<0$ when $0<x<12$.

The denominator $\log_3 x$ is $<0$ for $0<x<1$ and $>0$ for $x>1$.
For the fraction to be $>0$, numerator and denominator must have the same sign:
\[
0<x<1 \quad(\text{both negative}),\qquad \text{or}\qquad x>12 \quad(\text{both positive}).
\]
Thus the solution set is
\[
(0,1)\cup(12,+\infty).
\]

\subsection*{Answer}
$(0,1)\cup(12,+\infty)$ (option \textbf{D}).

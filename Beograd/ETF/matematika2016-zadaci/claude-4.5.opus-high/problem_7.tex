\section*{Problem 7}
Find the first derivative of the function $f(x) = \ln\dfrac{x^2 - 1 + \sqrt{x^4+1}}{x}$ at the point $x_0 = 1$.

\subsection*{Solution}
Let's rewrite the function:
\[f(x) = \ln\left(\frac{x^2 - 1 + \sqrt{x^4+1}}{x}\right) = \ln(x^2 - 1 + \sqrt{x^4+1}) - \ln x\]

Let $u = x^2 - 1 + \sqrt{x^4+1}$.

Then:
\[\frac{du}{dx} = 2x + \frac{4x^3}{2\sqrt{x^4+1}} = 2x + \frac{2x^3}{\sqrt{x^4+1}}\]

The derivative of $f(x)$ is:
\[f'(x) = \frac{1}{u} \cdot \frac{du}{dx} - \frac{1}{x}\]

At $x_0 = 1$:
\begin{align*}
u(1) &= 1 - 1 + \sqrt{1+1} = \sqrt{2}\\
\frac{du}{dx}\bigg|_{x=1} &= 2(1) + \frac{2(1)^3}{\sqrt{1+1}} = 2 + \frac{2}{\sqrt{2}} = 2 + \sqrt{2}
\end{align*}

Therefore:
\[f'(1) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \cdot (2 + \sqrt{2}) - 1 = \frac{2 + \sqrt{2}}{\sqrt{2}} - 1\]

Simplify:
\[\frac{2 + \sqrt{2}}{\sqrt{2}} = \frac{2}{\sqrt{2}} + \frac{\sqrt{2}}{\sqrt{2}} = \sqrt{2} + 1\]

So:
\[f'(1) = \sqrt{2} + 1 - 1 = \sqrt{2}\]

\subsection*{Answer}
$\sqrt{2}$ (option \textbf{D}).

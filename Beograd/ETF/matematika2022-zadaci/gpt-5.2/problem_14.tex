\section*{Problem 14}
Find the number of distinct real solutions of
\[
\sqrt{1-x}+\sqrt{1+x}-\sqrt{1-x^2}=\frac{5}{4}.
\]

\subsection*{Solution}
The domain is $-1\le x\le 1$.
Let
\[
a=\sqrt{1-x},\qquad b=\sqrt{1+x},
\]
so $a,b\ge 0$ and
\[
a^2+b^2=(1-x)+(1+x)=2.
\]
Also,
\[
\sqrt{1-x^2}=\sqrt{(1-x)(1+x)}=\sqrt{a^2b^2}=ab.
\]
The equation becomes
\[
a+b-ab=\frac{5}{4}.
\]
Denote $s=a+b$ and $p=ab$. Then the equation is $s-p=\frac54$, so $p=s-\frac54$.
But
\[
s^2=a^2+b^2+2ab=2+2p,
\]
hence $p=\frac{s^2-2}{2}$. Equate the two expressions for $p$:
\[
\frac{s^2-2}{2}=s-\frac54
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
2s^2-4s+1=0.
\]
Thus
\[
s=\frac{4\pm\sqrt{16-8}}{4}=1\pm\frac{\sqrt2}{2}.
\]
Since $a,b\ge 0$ and $a^2+b^2=2$, we must have $s=a+b\ge \sqrt2$.
Therefore only
\[
s=1+\frac{\sqrt2}{2}
\]
is possible.

\medskip
For this $s$, the pair $(a,b)$ is determined up to swapping, and swapping $(a,b)$ replaces $x$ by $-x$ (because $a^2=1-x$, $b^2=1+x$).
Here $s\ne 2$, so $a\ne b$ and $x\ne 0$. Hence there are exactly two solutions: $x=\pm x_0$.

\subsection*{Answer}
There are $2$ distinct real solutions (option \textbf{D}).

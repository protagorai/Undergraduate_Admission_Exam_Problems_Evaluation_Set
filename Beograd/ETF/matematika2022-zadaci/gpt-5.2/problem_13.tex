\section*{Problem 13}
An infinite decreasing geometric series with positive terms has sum $\dfrac{9}{2}$. If the sum of square roots of its terms equals $3$, find the common ratio.

\subsection*{Solution}
Let the first term be $a>0$ and common ratio $q$ with $0<q<1$.
Then
\[
\frac{a}{1-q}=\frac{9}{2}.
\]
The square roots form a geometric series with first term $\sqrt{a}$ and ratio $\sqrt{q}$:
\[
\sqrt{a}+\sqrt{aq}+\sqrt{aq^2}+\cdots=\frac{\sqrt{a}}{1-\sqrt{q}}=3.
\]
Let $r=\sqrt{q}\in(0,1)$. Then $\sqrt{a}=3(1-r)$, so
\[
a=9(1-r)^2.
\]
Substitute into the first sum, using $q=r^2$:
\[
\frac{9(1-r)^2}{1-r^2}
=\frac{9(1-r)^2}{(1-r)(1+r)}
=\frac{9(1-r)}{1+r}
=\frac{9}{2}.
\]
Cancel $9$:
\[
\frac{1-r}{1+r}=\frac{1}{2}
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
2(1-r)=1+r
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
r=\frac{1}{3}.
\]
Therefore
\[
q=r^2=\left(\frac{1}{3}\right)^2=\frac{1}{9}.
\]

\subsection*{Answer}
$\dfrac{1}{9}$ (option \textbf{A}).
